Academic Report: Reception Analysis

Myth Reworked
For my reception project, I reworked the myth of the Judgment of Paris and the Wedding of Peleus and Thetis (Lecture 7) through the lens of modern corporate bureaucracy. Using the format of leaked emails, chat logs, and HR incident reports, I satirized the transactional and petty nature of the Olympian gods. This format highlights how the Trojan War—a cataclysmic event—began not with a grand battle, but with a series of administrative failures, social snubs, and bribes.

Use of Course Concepts
The toggleable "Hermes Annotations" explicitly connect the creative choices to course material.
• The Email references Zeus’s "succession anxiety" regarding Thetis’s prophecy (that her son would be stronger than his father), which is why she was married off to a mortal (Lecture 7).
• The HR Report identifies Paris's choice not just as a preference, but as a violation of Xenia (Guest-Host friendship), noting that accepting Helen required him to violate the hospitality of Menelaus.
• The Bribe Table translates the goddesses' offers into corporate assets: Power (Hera), Kleos/Glory (Athena), and Physical Beauty (Aphrodite).

Creative Decisions
I chose the corporate format because it serves as a modern metaphor for the Aristocratic nature of the gods discussed throughout the course (Lecture 4). The gods often act like a dysfunctional family business, concerned with hierarchy and reputation rather than morality. The "Redacted" text feature emphasizes the theme of forbidden knowledge, while the interactive "Hermes Mode" allows me to embed my academic analysis directly into the creative experience.

/// CREATOR'S NOTE: THE SOURCE CODE OF MYTH ///

As a Computer Science student with a passion for web development, I approached this project by seeking the intersection between ancient narrative systems and modern digital systems. Mythology often functions remarkably like legacy code: it operates on strict logic (Fate/Prophecy), has rigid hierarchies (Olympus/Database Permissions), and contains fatal "bugs" (Tragedy/Hubris) that crash the system.

I chose to build this as an interactive "leaked database" because the medium is the message. By forcing the user to "hack" into the files and interact with elements like the "Hermes Annotations," I am simulating the work of a Classicist—digging through fragmented evidence to reconstruct meaning. This project allowed me to merge my technical skills with the course content, demonstrating that while the technology changes, the underlying stories of power and conflict remain constant.
